Getting There

The hike starts from Saltoluokta, which can be reached by a daily boat across lake Akkajaure from Kebnats. Bus #93 runs daily from Gallivare to Ritsem stopping on the way in Kebnats. Timetables for the bus can be found at The hike ends in Annonjalme which has daily boat services to Ritsem from where Bus #93 returns to Gallivare. Gallivare lies on the rail network with connections to Kiruna and Stockholm.

Description & Photos

Day 1: 6km

We arrived at Kebnats in the afternoon just in time to catch the 16:05 boat to Saltoluokta. The weather was pleasantly warm, and we were looking forward to a two-week adventure in the heart of Sarek. At the Saltoluokta mountain cabin we all raided the shop for our last bits of luxury, for some this was a chocolate bas for others it was a pint of Swedish lager. With our bellies full and spirit high we set off around 17:30 with the aim to get as far as we can. Our plan was to be able to catch the last boat over Sitojaure the following day. Our path from Saltoluokta follows the Kungsleden towards the south, which starts with an ascent onto the plateau east of Bietsavrre. Half an hour in and the birch forest starts to thin out opening the view back towards lake Langas, the lake we took the boat over just a few hours before. After a while, the terrain starts to flatten and the vegetation disappears, all that surrounds us is the open grassy pains of the Arctic. The Kungsleden is a marked trail that runs hundreds of kilometres through the Swedish mountains, it is well built making our advance easy and painless, there are planks over the marshes and well defines paths through the dirt left by the boots of the thousands of hikers that walk here every year. Little did we know how much we would miss these paths and planks in the bushes and swamps of the Rapadalen, but more on this later. Around 20:00 sunset is within an hour; it is time to look for a site to pitch the tents. We have amazing views towards the west with Lulep Gierkav towering over Bietsavvre. After a little bit of searching we find a nice site, just in time as heavy rain is approaching. We retire into our shelter and cook up our dinner while listening to sound of the raindrops hitting the canvas over our heads. It is cold, so we decide to call it a day and turn in for the night. I wake a few times overnight to sound of massive downpours, but our tents held up well.


The view towards Bietsavrre and Lulep Gierkav from the Kungsleden near Saltoluokta.

 

Day 2: 20km

The morning greets us with lovely sunshine, it is promising to be a good day. No time to rest, we have to catch the boat. We quickly pack up and try to dry the tents a bit in the morning sunshine, our plan is to have breakfast after an hour’s hike in the shelter hut at Avtsusjvagge. The going is easy as the Kungsleden propels us forward. We are getting hungry when, just in time, the tiny hut appears behind a hill. It is nice to shelter inside as the wind has picked up a bit. We cook some porridge and slurp our well-deserved cup of coffee. A fellow hiker joins us and offers us some smoked reindeer which is the local delicacy around these parts. Well rested and full of caffeine we continue our hike toward Sitojaure. This part of the hike is an easy descent towards the lake with just a bit of up and down, we get some distance behind us in no time. On the way, we see our first herd of reindeers grazing on the mountain vegetation, but they galop away as we approach. Nearing the lake, we descend into the birch forests and way ahead of time we arrive at Vaggevarasj, where the Sami run an on-demand boat service across the lake to Svijnne. The sami also operate a small shop where we of course buy a can of beer each which we delightfully drink with our lunch, a gourmet pack of 20p instant noodles. Around 17:00 we get taken to the other side of the lake by a little boat navigating the shallows of lake Sitojaure. We decide to continue south up the hill as we still have ample time till sunset. The Sami recommended that we fill up on water at the only bridge marked on the map, which we do as we know we won’t have any clear access to water till at least the following afternoon. Thus, with our backpacks full of water we continue up the hill towards Martevarasj. Now we start to feel our legs from the long morning hike and the extra 3kg of water on our backs is not helping. Just after the treeline we find a perfect opening to put up the tents, it was enough for today. We gather some woods and light a fire as we admire the amazing views of Sitojaure and the surrounding mountains.

Day 3: 13km

We again wake up to sunshine but now also aching muscles. There is no time to recover however, we want to take advantage of the weather and climb Skerffe today. So, we pack up and start our ascent on the steep slopes of Martevarasj, it is tough, but at least the prize of a breakfast granola waits for us on top. After an hour of tough climbing we arrive at the grassy plateau of Diaresoajvve and have our breakfast while soaking in the sunshine, it is our warmest day yet. We then hike south for another kilometre or so. This is where we wave goodbye to the Kungsleden and take a turn towards the west. The marked path is no more, and we have to navigate ourselves, but the terrain is very easy to walk on here, it is just a grassy plain with some scattered rocks. Keeping our elevation, we round the hill of Diaresoajvve and take aim at Skierffe which we can see towering over the Rapa delta, it is an impressive sight. It is at this point we enter into Sarek National Park, from here on there are no marked paths, and only a single bridge at the centre of the park so we will have to ford every river that crosses our paths. After making it over the false peak at 1106m we take a break and take our boots off for which our feet are very grateful. A large herd of reindeers surrounds us while we relax in the sun. Our plan is to leave our bags at the start of the last stretch up to the summit, as we will have to return the same way afterwards to continue our journey west. There is a small stream just before we turn south towards the summit where we freshen up and fill our bottles, we then dump our bags and hope we will be able to find them later. It feels amazing walking without 20kg on our backs, we just fly towards the peak at 1179m, the terrain is very rocky, but nothing can stop us. It takes us just 20 minutes to reach the summit where we get our first view of the beautiful Rapa delta. It is breath-taking! There is a mere 600m shear cliff below us on the southern edge of the peak, so we take good care and soak in the view. We spend a good hour up there taking hundreds of photos, we couldn’t have asked for better weather. We have no trouble finding our bags on the way down and so we take a turn to the west and descend through boggy terrain to the gently sloping hillside of Tjasskavarasj. We are hungry but I suggest we push on till we find a suitable spot for the tents, this is not a popular decision. After a while I give in and we have lunch, at around 5pm. Of course, around 5 minutes from our lunch spot we find level ground to pitch the tents. From here we have great views of the Rapadalen and the lonely lump of rock in the middle called Nammasj. Dinner is served around an hour after lunch and we retire into our tents early, it was a tough day.

Day 4: 6km

We wake up to the sound of rain hitting the tent, this does not motivate a quick start, we snooze and have a lazy morning. Poking our heads out of the tent reveals a foggy and cloudy Rapadalen which looks just as good as yesterday. The low clouds flow along the valley only disturbed by Nammasj in the middle causing them to spiral upwards, it is very nice to look at, from inside the tent that is. The rain is not letting down, so we decide to pack up and set off around 13:00. It is windy, rainy and cold, but we soldier on protected by our waterproof shells. We aim towards the saddle between Suarkitjahkka (1214m) and Niehter (1578m) while rounding Gierdogiesjtjahkka (1342m) from the south. The ascent is hard, the terrain is made up of huge boulders which are very slippery from the rain. We slowly advance towards the saddle, and after rounding the peak at 1342m the terrain momentarily becomes grassier just as the sun peaks through the clouds. The improvement in the terrain though is only temporary, continuing further up towards the saddle it again becomes very rocky. Luckily there is a large snowfield just ahead of us which we take advantage of and follow it up, right to the highest point on the saddle just north of the peak at 1214m. The descent toward the river Buovdajagasj greets us with more giant boulder fields making our advance very hard and slow. Arriving at the river reveals that there is still a thick layer of snow covering the water which we can see flowing lower down the slopes. We decide to cross the river over this snow bridge, but for extra safety we tie ourselves together to avoid falling down a crevasse. This was a good decision as just in the middle of the snowfield lay a big crack. We all managed to clear it and arrived safely at the other side. It was already late, and we were very tired from navigating the rough terrain, so we decided to set up camp at the first possible site, this being said, we were still surrounded by rocks, no grass in sight. Just as we were losing hope a small grassy patch shows up, just big enough to be able to pitch two tents. Jut in time, as the sun is setting, the wind is picking up and the temperature plummets. We quickly seek shelter in our sleeping bags and have no trouble falling asleep.

The clouds flowing along the Rapa valley in the morning disturbed by Nammasj on the right.

 


A boulderfield near the foothills of Gierdogiesjtjahkka with a view towards Skierffe and the Rapa delta.

 

Day 5: 5km

A cloudy morning, but no rain at least. We were planning a lazy day for today so we will only try to do about 5km, no rush. We set of around 13:00 and take aim at the saddle between the two unnamed peaks at 1112m and 1274m while keeping a bit closer to the northern peak not to lose too much altitude. This was not the best option as our path is blocked by a small wall of vertical rock, so we have to descend further into the saddle. This is where we get our first view of the upper parts of Rapadalen and the peak of Laddebakte (1537m) in the distance. The rocks here are interesting, they are not the usual random boulders but form large flat surfaces on which it is easy to walk, that is if you don’t slip. On one of these large flat rocks we sit down to take a rest and enjoy the amazing views towards the east with the Skarki massif to the right and the Bielloriehppe to the left. We continue towards the river Lulep Vassjajagasj while trying not to descend too much as we suspect that the river would bee easier to cross higher up. We think we are getting close, but we still cannot see the river, the reason for this we soon find out. The river has cut a deep gorge into the mountainside, so we have to descend the steep banks in order to cross to the other side. At lest the height makes it easier to pick a spot where to cross, we identify a region largely free of rapids and make our way down towards it. The river is too deep to cross with boots on, so we decide to have lunch before attempting the crossing with our brand-new neoprene socks. It is 16:00 and cloudy, on the menu is again our extra-special instant noodles, this time in chicken flavour. The crossing went without mishaps, it was quite refreshing. After swapping our footwear back to our boots, we scramble up the steep riverbank to be greeted by a large reindeer. It seemed to be the elder of the heard just a bit further back. The plan was to find a nice spot for the tents in the hillside and descend tomorrow into the valley following the river Alep Vassjajagasj. Soon, we find a good spot and set up camp and as it is relatively early, we treat ourselves to some makeshift seats made out of the flat layered rock scattered around our area. A few passing showers prompt us to go to bed.


A view of the Rapa river from the saddle below Radnik.

Day 6: 12km

We wake up reasonably early as there is a long day ahead of us, we want to make it to the foot of Laddebakte. It is cloudy, but only thin clouds so we hope the sun will prevail. We pack up quickly and choose not to have breakfast until we reach the large river Alep Vassjajagasj. We have two options, keep our altitude until we reach the river and follow it down to the valley or try to take a shortcut and start descending straight away while also heading toward the river. We chose the latter, which in hindsight was the wrong choice. We soon found ourselves in dense bushes trying to make our way down a steel hillside while avoiding branches and roots. We all fall at least 10 times before we reach level ground lower in the valley. Down in the valley we make our way north towards Alep Vassjajagasj surrounded by birch trees and dense undergrowth. We start to see the signs of large animas in the area, i.e. large piles of fresh poo. Is it a moose or is it a bear? We finally reach the river where we find a path heading down towards the Rapa river. As the river makes way for itself through the forest, we get a great view of the valley. Near the delta of the river as it flows into Rahpaadno, we finally stop and have breakfast, it is 1pm. The skies are now completely clear, and the sun is strong. We therefore decide to wash ourselves and our clothes in the river, this way they will have a chance to dry. It is nearly 3:30pm when we set off, we thoroughly enjoyed freshening up. First, we had to cross the river which meant taking out our neoprene socks again. I have read from various sources while planning the hike that if the water levels are low it is possible to walk on the banks of the Rapa river, so this is what we wanted to do. We started trying to make our way down to the shore, but our way was always blocked by deep watery swamps of dense vegetation. We therefore abandoned the plan and followed the path we could see in the forest. The path was not very well defined, we sometimes lost it and just had to follow rough directions. The going was extremely tough, it was a mix of dense vegetation and swamps. We kept losing the path and having to cut our way through the forest. It took us a long time to finally make it to the foot of Laddebakte. Sunset was nearing, but we were still low down in the forest, not somewhere to set up camp. Luckily, we found the path that lead up towards the saddle and Snavvavagge. Just as the sun set, we reached the treeline and found some nice spots for the tents. We quickly had dinner and went to bed.

Getting There

Jotunheimen National Park is located in the centre of Norway and is quite easily accessible from Oslo by bus. There is a seasonal bus service (line NW161) running from Oslo straight to Gjendesheim where this hike begins. The bus journey takes around 4 hours. More details including prices and timetables can be found on the bus operator's website , where you can also book tickets.

Description

Jotunheimen is the most mountainous region in Norway and contains Norway's highest peak. This hike takes you through some of the most stunning mountain scenery found in Europe. The going can sometimes be quite difficult due to the steep mountain slopes and very rocky terrain, especially at higher altitudes. Finding a spot to pitch up your tent can sometimes be challenging so it is always better to plan to camp a lower altitudes were there are more clear spaces without large boulders. Water is abundant throughout the national park. As the whole region is above the tree line there is little chance of gathering enough wood to make a fire, so a good gas stove and enough gas is essential.

Getting There

Northern Finland north of Rovaniemi generally has very little transport links. It is possible to catch a bus from Rovaniemi to the western edge of the national park, but this is still quite far from the eastern region where the highest peaks are. The starting point of this hike is thus only reachable by car and involves driving around 1-2h off the main road on a dirt track. There are many car rental options available in Rovaniemi.

Description

Urhro Kekkonen national park is a large area in north Finland bordering Russia. It is one of very few mountainous areas in Finland, but the heights are still a lot lower than in Sweden or Norway. Most valleys are still under the tree line. The whole region is basically a huge pine forest which may make navigation a bit tricky sometimes as the trees can cover out the features of the landscape. On the up side there is always enough wood to make a fire if the night gets chilly. The above features combined means that this national park is great for easy hikes with little elevation.

About Us

We are a couple of hiking enthusiasts based in the UK who migrate north every summer to explore some of the last untouched wildernesses in Europe. We hope the information on this page will help others who wish to do the same.
